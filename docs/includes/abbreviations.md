*[output]: The results JMP shows on screen after you run an analysis — the tables, graphs, and numbers JMP gives you back.
*[input]: The data you give JMP — usually a file (.csv or .jmp) you imported into the program.
*[variable]: A column in your dataset. Each variable measures one thing — age, weight, group assignment, etc.
*[observation]: A row in your dataset. Each observation is one participant, one patient, one record.
*[run]: To execute or perform — "run an analysis" means "click the button that makes JMP do the calculation."
*[fit]: To calculate the best mathematical line, curve, or model that matches your data — "fit a regression" means "find the line that best describes the relationship."
*[model]: A simplified mathematical description of how variables relate to each other.
*[coefficient]: A number in a model that tells you how strongly one variable affects another.
*[parameter]: A number that describes a population — μ, σ, π. The truth we're trying to estimate.
*[statistic]: A number that describes a sample — x̄, s, p̂. Our best guess at the parameter.
*[distribution]: How the values of a variable are spread out — what values are common, what values are rare.
*[sample]: The participants you actually studied — the data you collected.
*[sample size]: How many participants are in your sample, written as n.
*[estimate]: A guess at a population value, calculated from a sample. Also used as a verb — "to estimate" means to use sample data to guess a parameter.
*[random]: Determined by chance, with each outcome having a known probability — NOT "haphazard" or "weird."
*[significant]: In statistics, "statistically significant" means the result is unlikely to be due to chance. It does NOT mean "important" or "large."
*[hypothesis]: A specific claim about a population that we're testing using sample data.
*[null]: The "boring" hypothesis — that there is no effect, no difference, nothing interesting going on.
*[alternative]: The "interesting" hypothesis — that something IS happening (a difference, an effect, a relationship).
*[p-value]: The probability of seeing data as extreme as ours if the null hypothesis were actually true.
