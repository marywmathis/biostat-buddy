# Stats Vocabulary

## The words your textbook didn't define

Most stats classes use words that *sound* technical and assume you already know what they mean. You probably learned to nod at them and move on. That's not a personal failing — it's how the field talks.

This page lists the words you'll see throughout BioStat Buddy and biostatistics in general, with plain-language definitions. No shame, just clarity. Bookmark this page and come back any time a word feels slippery.

## The vocabulary

### Output
The results JMP shows on screen after you run an analysis — the tables, graphs, and numbers JMP gives you back. *"Look at the output"* means *"look at what JMP just calculated and displayed."*

### Input
The data you give JMP — usually a file (`.csv` or `.jmp`) you imported into the program. Input is what you start with; output is what you get after JMP analyzes it.

### Variable
A column in your dataset. Each variable measures one thing — age, weight, group assignment, blood pressure, etc. If your data is in a spreadsheet, every column header names a variable.

### Observation
A row in your dataset. Each observation is one participant, one patient, one record. If your study has 400 people, you have 400 observations.

### Run
To execute or perform an analysis. *"Run a t-test"* means *"click the buttons in JMP that make it perform a t-test."* Statisticians say "run" the way chefs say "make."

### Fit
To calculate the best mathematical line, curve, or model that matches your data. *"Fit a regression"* means *"find the line that best describes the relationship in your data."* When statisticians say a model "fits well," they mean it represents the data accurately.

### Model
A simplified mathematical description of how variables relate to each other. *"Build a model"* doesn't mean assembling something physical — it means writing down a formula that approximates how things in the real world connect.

### Coefficient
A number in a model that tells you how strongly one variable affects another. In the equation $y = 5x + 2$, the coefficient of $x$ is 5. In stats, coefficients usually show up in regression output and tell you the size of an effect.

### Parameter
A number that describes a *population* — like $\mu$ (population mean), $\sigma$ (population standard deviation), or $\pi$ (population proportion). Parameters are the *truth we're trying to estimate* but rarely get to measure directly.

### Statistic
A number that describes a *sample* — like $\bar{x}$ (sample mean), $s$ (sample standard deviation), or $\hat{p}$ (sample proportion). Statistics are *our best guesses* at the parameters, calculated from data we actually collected.

### Distribution
How the values of a variable are spread out — what values are common, what values are rare. The "shape" of your data when you graph it. A distribution can be normal (bell-shaped), skewed (lopsided), or other shapes.

### Sample
The participants you actually studied — the data you collected. Different from "the population," which is the larger group you're trying to learn about.

### Sample size
How many participants are in your sample. Always written as $n$. *"What's your sample size?"* = *"How many people did you study?"*

### Estimate
A guess at a population value, calculated from a sample. Used as both a noun ("the estimate is 0.42") and a verb ("we estimated the population mean").

### Random
In statistics, "random" means *determined by chance, with each outcome having a known probability.* This is **NOT** the everyday meaning of random ("haphazard," "weird"). A random sample is one where each member of the population has a known chance of being selected — that's a precise mathematical concept, not a casual one.

### Significant
"Statistically significant" means the result is unlikely to be due to chance alone, given your assumptions. It does **NOT** mean "important," "large," or "meaningful in real life." A statistically significant result can still be tiny and clinically irrelevant. The word is heavily abused — even by professionals.

### Hypothesis
A specific claim about a population that we're testing using sample data. Hypotheses come in pairs (null and alternative).

### Null hypothesis
The "boring" hypothesis — that there is no effect, no difference, nothing interesting going on. Usually written $H_0$. *"The drug has no effect"* is a null hypothesis.

### Alternative hypothesis
The "interesting" hypothesis — that something IS happening. A difference, an effect, a relationship. Usually written $H_1$ or $H_a$. *"The drug works"* is an alternative hypothesis.

### p-value
The probability of seeing data as extreme as ours (or more extreme) *if the null hypothesis were actually true.* Small p-value = "this would be surprising if there were no effect" = evidence against the null.

### Inference
The whole process of using sample data to make claims about a population. Every confidence interval, hypothesis test, and regression in this course is a form of inference.

### Standard deviation
A number that measures how spread out the values of a variable are. Bigger SD = more spread, smaller SD = tighter cluster around the mean. Sample standard deviation is $s$; population is $\sigma$.

### Standard error
The standard deviation of a *statistic* — a measure of how much your sample estimate would jiggle around if you repeated the study. Different from standard deviation, even though they sound similar.

### Confidence interval
A range of plausible values for a population parameter, computed from your sample. *"95% confident the true mean is between 70 and 75"* is a confidence interval claim.

### Regression
A method for describing the relationship between variables — usually how one or more "predictor" variables affect an "outcome" variable. Linear regression fits a straight line; other types fit curves or non-linear shapes.

### Correlation
A number ($r$ for a sample, $\rho$ for a population) that describes how strongly two variables move together. Ranges from -1 (perfect negative relationship) to +1 (perfect positive relationship), with 0 meaning no linear relationship.
